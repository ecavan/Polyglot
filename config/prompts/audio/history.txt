This content is history-domain narrated or conversational audio (documentaries, lectures, history podcasts). Expect formal, sometimes dramatic narration and dense proper nouns. Listen carefully for historical figures and dynasties (Charlemagne, the Habsburgs, the Ming, Genghis Khan, Eleanor of Aquitaine), battles and sieges (Hastings, Agincourt, Stalingrad, Thermopylae), treaties (Westphalia, Versailles, Tordesillas), places and polities (Constantinople, Mesopotamia, the Holy Roman Empire, Gaul), eras (the Renaissance, the Bronze Age, the Reconquista), Latin and foreign terms (casus belli, imperium, polis), and dates/centuries ("the fourth century BC", "1066", "the 1700s"). Transcribe these precisely; do not normalize unfamiliar names into ordinary English words.

When translating to Québécois French, keep personal names, dates, and numbers verbatim, but use the established French conventional name wherever one exists: "William the Conqueror" -> "Guillaume le Conquérant", "the Roman Empire" -> "l'Empire romain", "the Battle of Hastings" -> "la bataille de Hastings", "Charles V" -> "Charles Quint", "the Crusades" -> "les Croisades", "the Middle Ages" -> "le Moyen Âge". Spell out centuries in French ("the 14th century" -> "le 14e siècle"). Translate standard historiographical terms (empire, siege, reign, dynasty) into French; keep Latin/foreign phrases as-is.

Speaker roles are usually a single narrator for documentaries (one consistent label), or a host plus one or more guests/experts for conversational shows — label them stably. Pitfalls: misheard foreign place names, archaic or Latin vocabulary, and dramatic narration with long pauses or emphatic pacing that can fragment lines — keep each transcribed line coherent.
